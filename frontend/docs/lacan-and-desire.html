As M. N. Epstein writes: "Desire often presents itself as straightforward, open, seeking the swiftest satisfaction. 'I desire such-and-such, and so it shall be.' In reality, like a word with a loophole, desire has its reservation. The secret of desire is that it both wants and does not want its own satisfaction; it wants satisfaction with a delay, so as to preserve itself as desire. Desire constantly walks along the edge of enjoyment, trying to reach that edge while at the same time not spilling over it, not exhausting itself prematurely."

First, desire is the desire to be the object of the other's desire, that is, the desire for recognition by the other.

Second, the subject desires by means of the other, that is, desires from the other's point of view. Consequently, "the object of a person's desire is essentially an object desired by someone else" [2, p. 12]. What makes an object desirable is not some quality intrinsic to it, but the very fact that another desires it.

Thus, the desire of the Other makes objects equivalent and interchangeable; "An object can become equivalent to another as a result of the influence produced by this intermediary phenomenon, which creates the possibility for objects to be exchanged or compared. This process tends to nullify the special significance of any particular object, but, at the same time, it allows us to suppose the existence of an infinite number of objects" [2, p. 12]. Consequently, desire is not any private matter; it is always constituted in a dialectical relation with the perceived desires of other subjects.

Third, desire is always desire for something else, since it is impossible to desire what you already possess. Desire is characterized by an infinite continuous process of deferral; as soon as the object of desire is attained, it ceases to be desirable, and the subject's desire chooses for itself another object.

Fourth, the fundamental desire is the incestuous desire for the mother as the primordial other. The mother is the first person to occupy the place of the other, and at first the child is entirely at the disposal of her desire. Only when the Father, by castrating the mother, joins desire to the law does the subject free himself from subjection to the whims of her desire.

It should be noted that the term "other" in Lacan splits into the "little other" ("other") and the "big Other" ("Other"). "The little other is an illusory other, in reality being a reflection and projection of the Ego. It is simultaneously both the Double and the Mirror Image. Consequently, the little other is fully inscribed in the order of the imaginary. The big other represents a radical alterity, an otherness, unassimilable through identification and therefore surpassing the illusory otherness of the imaginary. The Other is inscribed in the order of the symbolic, since Lacan compares its radical alterity with language and the law. Thus, the big Other is the individualized form of the symbolic for each subject [3].

Lacan held that the formation of the human world as such occurs in a primordial rivalry with others. The struggle for recognition, the struggle for desire unfolds in the dialectic of relations with the other. The Hegelian dialectic of master and slave consists in the fact that the master deprives the slave of his enjoyment, takes possession of the object of desire as the object of the slave's desire, obtains recognition from the slave, but in doing so loses his own independence. The master has no way to overcome his alienation by his own efforts. According to Lacan, the one who has won enjoyment becomes a complete idiot, capable of nothing but enjoyment, whereas the one deprived of enjoyment preserves all his humanity. The slave recognizes the master, and so he has the possibility of obtaining recognition from him in turn. Thus, for ages he wages a struggle for worthy recognition. The slave is made to work, and he gains the possibility of perceiving himself through the creations of his own hands. The master remains in dependence, while the slave prepares himself for independence. He gains the chance to be recognized in his labor. "And so, desire is grasped as the desire of the other, not because the other holds the key to the desired object, but because behind the desire for some object, whether a thing or a person, lies the desire to be recognized by the other. It is the desire to be desired. The desire for the other's desire. Thus, behind the desire to receive milk already stands the desire to receive recognition, the mother's love. This recognition essentially confirms existence. To be recognized means to exist" [4, p. 15].

The other is the one who sees me, and this is quite enough to set in motion a struggle conditioned by considerations of prestige, within which desire turns out to be implicated.

According to Lacan, desire is the relation of being to the lack that is the lack of being as such, by means of which the existent exists. This lack lies beyond everything that can reveal it. Desire lies at the origin of everything that makes a being animate. By virtue of this very lack the being turns out to be existing. It is precisely by virtue of this lack that, in the experience of desire, the being comes to the experience of its own I in its relations with being. It is precisely in pursuit of that beyond, which is nothing, that it returns again and again to the experience of itself as a being conscious of itself. "In reality, relations between human beings are established without reaching the field of consciousness. The original organization of the human world is carried out by desire, desire as an unconscious factor" [6, p. 317].

What matters is not the object toward which desire is directed, but the cause of its arising, which Lacan calls the object a ("a" from the French "autre" — other). Since desire, according to Lacan, is always the desire of the other, he calls it the object a — the object-cause of the desire of the other.

The innervation key — doors (Oles Manyuk)

The innervation key — switching.

Doors as a passage between realities. Without a door the passage is physical death.

Lacan mentioned that doors are a paradoxical object: all their properties are exclusively relative; they arise only when there are narrow realities between which they are situated.

Doors are a connecting segment of reality that does not merely create a connection but actualizes the mutual permeability of two realities (transcending in relation to both).

Doors work exclusively symbolically — they introduce into the dense reality of images the slit-gap of lack, into which no image can be inserted. This is the function of passage.

In the absence of doors, the meaning of these doors is realized through the subject himself. The subject himself turns out to be the door. And this door is at the same time death.

The vase as signifier (Lacan)

"This nothing in particular, which characterizes the vase in its signifying function, is embodied in the form that characterizes the vase as such. For the vase creates precisely the void, thereby opening for the first time the prospect of filling it. The empty and the full enter the world through the vase — in itself the world knows nothing of the sort. Only by means of the vase, this man-made signifier, do the empty and the full enter the world as such, carrying within themselves the same meaning."
