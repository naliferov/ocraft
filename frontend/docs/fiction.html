Gorchev — "Princes"

Anyone can become a Prince. The Prince's main function is to carry his chosen one off into another, happier world (an improvement in material circumstances or an "atmosphere of high spirituality"). The moment that function is fulfilled, he stops being a Prince. What remains is a household pet: it snores, yawns, scratches its belly. If he wants to be a Prince again, he can find another woman. As long as he's alive, the number of attempts is unlimited.

(The whole of Kernberg's "Love Relations: Normality and Pathology" in a single short story — in Manyuk's opinion.)

Gorchev — "Time"

An old woman asked for the time from a clock, not from a phone. "Those fidgety electrons, about which the only thing known for certain is that they are always somewhere other than where they're supposed to be." Electronic time tells you only how late you already are. Real time doesn't skip about: it's like fog — in the hollows it lies still. Grandmother's wall clock is a mechanism for producing slow, slow time. And if you walk into an empty house in an abandoned village, there will always be a broken clock there: "time has run out and eternity has begun."
