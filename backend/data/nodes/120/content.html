What matters is not to lose? From the standpoint of Chinese philosophy, the only correct goal is one that grows out of reliance on the holistic potential of a concrete situation and serves as its fullest possible expression.

All other goals are unstable fantasies that, even once realized, carry within them an enormous risk of negative tendencies in the future. Why? For one simple reason — such goals are an act of violence against nature, against the natural order of things.

The correct strategy from the standpoint of growth and development sounds like this: Find and lose Strive toward the future? Strive to empty out the past before moving into the future

To be here and now? Meanwhile, serious changes are always unexpected; they always enter the present moment from the outside. This includes, among other things, the notorious "black swans" that Nassim Taleb wrote about. It is important to expand your perception beyond the present moment, not to fixate on it, but to remain open to change. Be everywhere and always.
What matters is to be in the flow of change, not in a single present moment

Not to bother with trifles? serious changes begin with very insignificant phenomena, which the more insignificant they appear on the outside, the more significant the inner potential they carry within them.

It is important to see in the faint stirring of the grass the signs of an approaching storm: what appears significant is in a stage of decline, while the significant hides in the small and the insignificant — so it is said in the treatise "Guan Yinzi".

To follow your dream? Striving for a dream makes one dependent on one's own fantasies, whereas for genuine development a person must be independent and free from oneself. For it is precisely the image of oneself that closes off our own possibilities to us.

Likewise, there is no need to fight against the inevitable. For struggling with the inevitable places a person in a position of dependence on it.

Do not follow your dream and do not fight the inevitable, but pass between them!

in Chinese art the future is not created but revealed. The future is revealed only when a person relies not on abstract ideas but on the potential of the holistic situation. In other words, when a person undertakes no arbitrary actions but, recognizing the holistic potential, creates the conditions for its most favorable unfolding

This is precisely that "non-action" that Laozi and other philosophers of traditional China wrote about. As you can see, there is no mysticism here at all. What is more, non-action by no means rejects planning and control. But! It allows for them within strictly defined periods of time — only when they are necessary. And the necessity of control arises only at the moments when ever-changing processes pass into a brief phase of relative rest. In business, the main thing is "to be able to extend one's
intention far beyond the eight directions of the world"

And now I will return to what the Chinese tradition says about this, and how. For traditional Chinese humanistic culture, the world is not a collection of things, more or less stable, but a totality of processes, where the principal principle is transformation.

Such a vision allows one not only to recognize and accept the instability, the fluidity of the world, but also to set priorities in a completely distinctive way. For what is the main thing in the world of things, or, in the words of one of the founders of synergetics, the Nobel laureate physical chemist Ilya Prigogine, the "world-museum"? It is the immutability of things.

Let me clarify: of course, things change, but in their particulars, while at their core they remain the same. The world of immutable things suggests a corresponding strategy for dealing with it: to seize, to appropriate, to multiply. Hence the constant tilting of the scales toward "having" (this refers to Erich Fromm's famous dilemma of "to have or to be").

Chinese thought says — betting on "having" turns into ruin, for it is inevitable that what was acquired will be lost, and if you build your whole life on the foundation of "having" — expect trouble. And so the optics of Chinese philosophy open up another world, in which there are no things, but only their changing interactions and the properties of those interactions.

The highest horizon is emptiness, formlessness, or, in Chinese terminology, Wuji — the Great Limitless. There is no predetermined direction; everything is decided by the horizon of events. The wider it is, the freer it is from constraints, the more — multivariate! — possibilities for development events will receive

In the general case, in the context of the Chinese art of governance, yang — the masculine principle — is creativity, innovation, while yin — the feminine principle — is realization and adaptation.

Aurora Chen Yongtai said that the main secret of successful management is to notice two things in time: 1) when the introduction of innovations hampers everyday management and a creative approach must be applied to optimize management processes, and 2) when everyday management processes stifle innovation and management must be reoriented toward creating the most favorable conditions for the development of innovation. In this way the Taiwanese businessman demonstrated his commitment to the principle that "excess helps deficiency".
