A creative system is a generator of a possibility space. You don't design levels, cars, trees — you design the space inside which they arise. You experience it as "the creative act" not in the elements, but in the space they give rise to. The main thing is to choose the minimum of primitives such that the space comes out maximal yet meaningful — not just large.

Generativity lives in the relations between elements, not in the elements. Lego has a few types of elements, but the space is large; they connect via one universal connector. Go has one piece and one rule, yet the game tree is enormous. The power is in the joining interface, in the rules of interaction, not in the richness of detail. That's why a minimal system can be more powerful than a rich one: you push complexity out of the elements and into the connections.

What are the elements in such a system? 7 dimensions: Atoms — irreducible units. How many types? Homogeneous (a voxel, a stone in Go) or heterogeneous (Lego, chess)? Do they carry state? Do they carry behavior? Homogeneity + scale often beats variety.

Connectors — how atoms join. The most underrated and most important part. A universal connector (a peg, adjacency on a grid) → combinatorial explosion. A typed one (port inputs/outputs, as in node editors) → directed structure, a pipeline. This is exactly where generativity is born.

Rules (dynamics) — what happens after joining. Static (it just sits there, like a drawing) or dynamic (it works — Game of Life, Factorio, redstone). Dynamics turn "placing things" into "programming" and yield emergence — behavior of the whole that you didn't put in.

Substrate / topology — the arena where the elements live. A grid? A plane? A graph? Nesting? This is a primitive choice people make without noticing. Your "rooms with passages" are a graph of spaces, where each room is its own substrate. Topology determines which adjacencies and flows are even possible.

Flow — does something flow through the structure? Signals, data. If so, the structure becomes a program, and the creative act becomes designing transformations of the flow. This is where Factorio lives. But flow isn't mandatory — without it you're in a space of static compositions.

Constraints / scarcity — limits that make choice meaningful (few elements, little room, cost, conservation laws). Without constraints there's no tension, only arrangement. Stravinsky: "the more constraints, the more freedom." A blank canvas paralyzes; a frame forces you to invent.

Feedback — does the creator see what their composition does? Instant feedback (the machine spinning right before your eyes) makes the space explorable rather than blind.

The possibility space itself is not an eighth element but a consequence of the first seven. Its size, navigability, and capacity to surprise can't be designed directly — they flow from the choices in 1–7.

What distinguishes a good system from a heap of junk? There's a difference between a combinatorial space and a meaningful space. Random pixels have a gigantic combinatorial space — and almost all of it is meaningless. Go has a gigantic space where almost every position is meaningful. You don't need a large number of combinations — you need structured generativity, where the space is mostly interesting. And here's what does that: rules concentrate meaning, cutting away chaos and condensing the meaningful. Without rules you have entropy; with rules — a language.

Hence two quality criteria for primitives: Orthogonality. The best primitives act independently, so combinations multiply rather than add. If A and B do different things and combine freely, you get A×B behaviors; if they overlap — redundancy. Each new primitive must buy a new dimension of expressiveness. You'll recognize this from your architectural work — the same principle as good API design.

Basis. An elegant minimal system is one where the primitives form a basis of the space: minimal, independent, spanning everything. Like the basis of a vector space. This is your north star: not "a set of nice objects," but a basis of the creative space — the smallest set whose combinations express everything you want to allow. Finding the basis is the whole design problem.

The answer to the question you kept turning over the whole time: where does the interest come from? Interest scales exactly with how much the space exceeds what you explicitly put in.

A static placement editor gives the weakest form — the aesthetics of arrangement, interesting while you're drawing. Add rules and flow, and two more, inexhaustible forms open up: mastery (the space is deeper than you, and you spend years finding better solutions) and surprise (the system makes a third thing out of two that you didn't foresee). Two people solve the same thing differently — and the solution has style.

By the way, you already do this intuitively in music. Alva Noto is literally a minimal creative system: the click and the sine as the basis, structure as the generator, infinite variation of micro-details from a few rules over time. The same principle, only the substrate is time, not space.

The heart is the basis plus the rules from which the space becomes greater than the sum of the rooms.

Technical implementation

How to build this possibility space as a signal-driven node graph. The philosophy (basis, orthogonality, constraints, Alva Noto, meaningful-vs-combinatorial space) is in the conceptual part above and isn't repeated here.

In short (TL;DR)

  • Build it as a pull-based, dirty-flagged dataflow graph of typed nodes that carry signals. The TouchDesigner lesson: the right minimal set of primitives is a few data families (image/texture, channel/control-signal, geometry, data/text) with explicit converter nodes between them. The signal is the medium, the node is an almost-pure function with ports, state, and behavior.
  • Drive everything from a single global transport/clock, built on Web Audio's AudioContext.currentTime using the "two clocks" pattern (a lookahead scheduler); FFT/onset/beat analysis nodes feed the graph. Quantize visual events (image changes, cuts) to the musical grid so the transformation lands on the beat — this is exactly how BPM-sync works in Resolume/VDMX.
  • Use a hybrid substrate: free 2D coordinates for layout (an infinite canvas in the tldraw/Obsidian style) plus a typed graph for logic, with "portals" as a separate edge type with teleport/navigation semantics (not literal nesting). On the web stack that's Rete.js or a custom store for the graph + WebGL (regl/three/PixiJS/hydra-synth) for the transformation layer + Web Audio for the clock.

Key takeaways

  1. Every serious node-based creative tool converges on a small set of typed data families plus converter operators, not on a flat "soup of nodes." The clearest example is TouchDesigner: exactly seven operator families — TOP (textures/images, GPU), CHOP (channels: audio, control, animation signals), POP/SOP (points/geometry), DAT (text/tables/scripts), MAT (materials), COMP (components/containers), with explicit cross-family converters (TOP-to-CHOP, SOP-to-DAT). This typed design is the most important architectural lesson for the goal of "signals/data flowing through the graph."
  2. The dominant high-performance computation model is pull-based (demand-driven) with dirty-flag propagation, not push. TouchDesigner is a "pull system": a change to an input value merely marks a node dirty; it doesn't "cook" until a sink (display/output) asks for the data. Blender rewrote the geometry-nodes evaluator as a lazy-function system that "computes only the data actually needed." This is exactly what a real-time tool needs: the output (the rendered frame) pulls every frame, and only the dirty subgraphs are recomputed.
  3. The CS theory underneath all of this has long been settled and gives precise rules. FRP/reactive systems achieve glitch-freedom by topologically sorting the dependency DAG. Synchronous dataflow languages (Lustre) and the SDF model formalize multi-rate scheduling — the theory behind the mismatch of audio-rate / frame-rate / event-rate. The hard rule for feedback: every cycle in an otherwise-acyclic dataflow graph must contain at least one unit-delay element (a frame-delay register / pre in Lustre / z⁻¹ in DSP), otherwise — deadlock.
  4. A small set of primitives yields a large meaningful (not merely combinatorial) space only when the primitives are orthogonal/composable and the rules concentrate meaning. L-systems build complex structures from an alphabet + rules + axiom; the concept of expressive range from PCG is a tool to measure and tune the generative space; the "Kaleidoscope Effect" warns: high combinatorial capacity at low perceptual diversity is boring. (Stravinsky's constraints principle and the aesthetics of Alva Noto / Ryoji Ikeda are in the conceptual part.)
  5. A graph can be both a logical structure and a navigable space, and "portals" are cleanly modeled as a typed edge with teleport semantics. Spatial hypertext (VIKI) and zoomable UIs (Pad++) showed that free spatial placement + links are enough for emergent structure and navigation. Modern infinite-canvas engines (tldraw, Obsidian Canvas) store everything as flat JSON records (nodes with x,y + edges/bindings) in a reactive store — which maps directly onto a coordinates+graph hybrid.

Details

1. Architecture of node-based tools — primitives and execution models

TouchDesigner (reference architecture). Operators ("OP") are the nodes; they "cook" (compute) and yield data. The key idea is families: TOP (2D textures, GPU); CHOP (channels — sequences of samples for audio, animation, control signals, timing, logic, device I/O — this is your "signal" primitive); SOP/POP (3D geometry/points); DAT (text, tables, scripts, GLSL); MAT (materials/shaders); COMP (containers with sub-networks and UI). Important: data doesn't flow between families silently — there are explicit converter operators (TOP-to-CHOP turns pixels into channels, SOP-to-DAT turns points into a table).

The computation model — pull-based with dirty-flags. Changing a parameter marks a node dirty; it "cooks" only when a downstream consumer (ultimately the display pulling a frame) asks for data. For a node to "cook," two conditions are needed: (1) there's a request to cook and (2) there's a reason (it's dirty). A few special nodes (Render TOP, device-In) "always cook," because monitoring all their inputs is more expensive than recomputing — a pragmatic exception worth copying.

Max/MSP & Pure Data — the two-rates lesson. Max distinguishes two levels of time: control rate (the event scheduler, a 1 ms tick) and audio/signal rate (44.1/48 kHz, computed in vectorized blocks — the "signal vector size"). Control messages travel as discrete events through patch cords; audio signal connections compile into a separate signal graph that runs at audio rate in blocks. Bridge objects (sig~, snapshot~, line~) explicitly convert between rates. Lesson: treat audio-rate analysis (FFT, envelope follower) as a separate rate domain from the frame-rate (60 fps) visual graph and the event-rate (beat/onset triggers) domain, with explicit sample/hold bridges between them.

vvvv / Houdini / Blender — three adjacent lessons. vvvv: the central structure is the spread (an immutable dynamic 1-D array), nodes auto-process spreads element-wise — this is data parallelism (a grid of 25 trees) without explicit loops. Houdini VOPs: the visual graph compiles into executable VEX — meaning the graph and the compiled kernel are two views of the same computation. Blender (3.0+): the "lazy-function" evaluator computes only the needed inputs/outputs (a Switch computes only the chosen branch); the node tree splits into two flows — data flow (geometry) and function flow (fields computed per-element, lazily). The split of "value-per-element vs the structure through which they flow" is a clean model.

Cables.gl — the closest web analog. Patch-based: operators ("Ops") connected by typed ports and links; changing an operator's output passes the value to the connected inputs and may trigger the receiver. It adds explicit trigger ports for imperative execution order on top of the dataflow — a push/trigger hybrid. It compiles to WebGL/WebGPU, with custom Ops in JS. Its object model — core_patch.js, core_op.js, core_port.js, core_link.js — is a clean minimum worth emulating.

Hydra (live-coding video synth) — the function-chain model. Inspired by analog modular synths: you start with a source (osc(), shape(), noise(), src()) and chain transforms (.rotate(), .kaleid(), .modulate(), .blend(), .diff()), finishing with .out(o0). Architecturally — 4 source buffers (s0–s3) and 4 output buffers (o0–o3), each a WebGL framebuffer; each chained function "corresponds to a fragment-shader snippet," and the chain compiles into a single GLSL fragment shader. Output buffers can sample one another — src(o0).modulate(o1) — hence feedback and the "hall-of-mirrors." The most directly portable model: a small set of source/geometry/color/blend/modulate, composed by chaining, compiled into a single shader. hydra-synth is available as an npm module you can embed.

The minimal taxonomy of node types everyone converges on. The recurring categories: Sources/Generators (produce data with no input: oscillator, noise, image, camera, constant), Filters/Transforms (one-to-one: rotate, blur, color, math), Combiners (many-to-one: blend, composite, merge, modulate), Sinks/Outputs (pull the graph: render to screen/buffer/device), Converters (bridges between types). Adopt exactly these five roles as node super-types.

2. Dataflow and reactive computation models (theory for the implementer)

Push vs pull. Push (data-driven) propagates eagerly forward from changed inputs; pull (demand-driven) recomputes lazily when an output is requested. Conal Elliott ("Push-pull FRP") proposes combining them: push for discrete events, pull for continuous behaviors. The pragmatic choice (as in TouchDesigner) is pull-per-frame at sinks + dirty-flags to prune recomputation, plus push triggers for discrete musical events (beats/onsets).

Glitch-freedom via topological order. A glitch is a temporary inconsistency where a node updates on a mix of old and new input values. It's avoided by processing the dependency graph in topological order. Mandatory if a node depends on two paths from a shared ancestor (a diamond) — typical in audio-reactive graphs where a single analysis node branches out.

Lustre (a synchronous dataflow language). Every variable is a flow = an infinite sequence of values + a clock. The synchrony hypothesis: outputs are conceptually computed in zero logical time. Key operators: pre (previous value), -> (initialization/"followed-by"), when (sample onto a slower clock), current (hold the last value back onto a faster clock). Lustre compiles the entire declarative graph into one sequential reaction loop (a Mealy machine: read inputs → compute → write outputs) by topologically sorting the equations. This is exactly the discipline worth copying: the per-frame tick is one "reaction."

SDF & multi-rate scheduling. SDF (Lee & Messerschmitt) fixes the number of tokens each actor produces/consumes per firing, which makes it possible to compute a static periodic schedule at compile time. Multirate is modeled naturally: a downsampler consumes N produces 1, an upsampler the reverse; the repetition vector says how many times each actor fires per period to balance the rates. The graph is consistent only if such a vector exists. Takeaway: if you want determinism and a precomputed schedule, fix the nodes' I/O rates; if you want dynamics, go Kahn-style.

KPN (Kahn Process Networks). A deterministic concurrent runtime: sequential processes communicate via unbounded FIFO channels with blocking reads and non-blocking writes, with no way to check a channel for tokens without consuming them. These constraints make the network deterministic — the sequence of messages doesn't depend on process speeds or execution order. This is a theoretical guarantee that the graph yields the same result regardless of node evaluation order — valuable for reproducibility and save/replay. (SDF is a statically-scheduled special case of KPN.)

Feedback/cycles — the unit-delay rule. The main theorem: every directed cycle must contain at least one delay element (one initial token / a frame-delay register). In Lustre the delay is pre; in DSP it's z⁻¹; in Max/MSP audio feedback must go through tapin~/tapout~. For the engine: detect cycles; legalize them only if they pass through an explicit "Feedback"/"Delay" node that reads the previous frame's value. Hydra does this implicitly: the output buffer o0 loops back into its own chain via src(o0) — the previous frame's framebuffer.

3. Audio-reactive / rhythm-driven visual systems

The analysis pipeline. Real-time audio → features → visual parameters. Standard features: FFT/spectral analysis (Web Audio AnalyserNode.getByteFrequencyData / Tone.js Tone.Analyser), split into musically meaningful bands (bass/mid/treble); RMS/amplitude (envelope follower); onset detection (spectral flux); beat/tempo (BPM) tracking via autocorrelation of the onset function. The FFT trade-off: more bins = a longer time window = more latency; small FFTs (32–128) react faster and are good for beat detection, large ones (1024–2048) give a finer spectrum.

The global clock / transport — the architectural cornerstone. On the web the mandatory pattern is the "two clocks" (Chris Wilson, "A Tale of Two Clocks"): the precise audio clock is AudioContext.currentTime (seconds, sample-accurate, a separate thread), but you schedule via a setTimeout/worker lookahead loop. Rule of thumb: ~100 ms lookahead, a 25 ms interval; each call schedules the next 100 ms. The canonical loop: while (nextNoteTime < audioContext.currentTime + scheduleAheadTime) { scheduleNote(...); advance(); }. The callback period < lookahead — for robustness. Libraries: WAAClock, Tone.js Transport. Architecture: a single Transport/Clock node owns the BPM and emits quantized ticks (16th, beat, bar, phrase); the rest of the nodes subscribe to it. This is Resolume's model.

Tempo-synced transformation and changing images on the beat. A global BPM (tapped, entered, or from Ableton Link / MIDI clock), with clips/effects quantized to beats/bars/phrases (4 beats = bar, 8 bars = phrase). For the requirement "cycle images on the beat": a cycle/sequencer node subscribes to the clock's beat events and advances an index over a list of images, tying the changes to the musical grid. VDMX patches LFOs/envelopes "anything to anything" — the modular control-routing model you want.

Ableton Link — the compatibility standard: it synchronizes BPM and phase between apps on the local network. Worth supporting so the tool latches directly onto your Ableton sessions rather than going through audio analysis.

Feedback effects. Video feedback (a framebuffer reading its own previous frame, transformed) is the foundation of generative video aesthetics. Implement it as a ping-pong pair of framebuffers (render into B while sampling A, swap) — this is the GPU realization of the unit-delay-in-a-cycle rule. Examples: o0 self-feedback in Hydra and the Feedback TOP in TouchDesigner.

4. Generative / emergent systems from minimal primitives

What makes a good basis of primitives (the technical echo of basis/orthogonality from the conceptual part). Concretely: the source/geometry/color/blend/modulate sets in Hydra and the TouchDesigner families are good bases, because any output feeds any input of the same type — orthogonal, composable, closed. Aim for the smallest set that spans your space.

L-systems and generative grammars (trees/buildings/structures). An L-system = an alphabet of symbols + rules (parallel rewriting) + an axiom; "complex structure from simple rules." Graph grammars — for non-linear structures. The recurring theme: parallel rewriting + a graphical interpretation step (turning the generated string into geometry) — this is itself a tiny node pipeline you can assemble from nodes.

Cellular automata and emergence. Minimal local rules → global complexity (Conway's Life, Wolfram CA) — the extreme case of "small rules, huge space"; excellent generator nodes for the visual layer.

Combinatorial vs meaningful space — the central design risk (the conceptual "meaningful space vs combinatorial," made measurable). Expressive range analysis is the standard method: generate many artifacts, score them with metrics (e.g. linearity/leniency/density), and build a 2-D histogram to see what the generator actually produces and what it doesn't. The "Kaleidoscope Effect": a generator can have huge numeric capacity and still be boring, because its output has "a hidden sameness that precludes surprise" — what matters to people is perceptual uniqueness. Takeaway: measure perceptual diversity, not combinatorial count; concentrate meaning with constraints/rules that shift the space toward expressive, readable outputs.

Constraints breed creativity (the through-line). (Stravinsky's principle and Alva Noto's aesthetics are in the conceptual part.) Complexity science says the same: "all order arises through constraint" (Ashby) — the rationale for a minimal set of primitives and a clocked, quantized system. This maps onto Ryoji Ikeda's aesthetics: maximum sensory experience from pure data — binary black-and-white, grids, sine waves (datamatics, test pattern). Factorio-style design is the interactive analog: simple components whose connections give rise to complexity — and that is exactly a node graph.

5. The graph as a navigable space + a spatial-coordinate hybrid

Spatial hypertext. VIKI "supports emergent qualities of structure" — the visual-spatial metaphor lets you express nuances of structure, even ambiguous or only-just-forming structure. The lesson: free coordinate placement is itself meaningful — don't cram everything into explicit edges; let clustering and position carry soft semantics, and explicit edges carry hard logic.

Zoomable UI (ZUI). Pad++ — one large information surface where documents are placed and scaled freely; navigation (pan/zoom/following a link) smoothly animates the view to the target. The insight: a spatial index culls invisible/too-small objects, and fps statistics drive level-of-detail — directly relevant to a performant infinite canvas. KidPad's quote on the advantage of animated portals over abrupt cuts: "with traditional hypertext it's like closing your eyes and opening them somewhere new; zooming lets you keep your eyes open."

Modern infinite-canvas engines (the implementation template). tldraw stores everything as flat JSON records in a reactive store (@tldraw/store); a shape has base props (x, y, rotation, opacity) plus typed props, and each type has a ShapeUtil class (rendering, geometry/hit-testing, interaction). Its binding system keeps relationships (e.g. a connection attached to a port) in sync as shapes move — and tldraw's Workflow starter kit implements exactly a node editor on the canvas: nodes are custom tldraw shapes, connections are also shapes but, via the binding system, hold onto concrete ports. An almost perfect reference for the coordinates+graph hybrid. Obsidian Canvas uses the open JSON Canvas format: a file with nodes[] (id, x, y, width, height, type) and edges[] (id, fromNode, fromSide, toNode, toSide) — a dead-simple portable schema you can adopt wholesale.

Portals / link-as-teleport — the clean model. Don't nest spaces matryoshka-style; model a portal as a typed edge (or a portal node) with the semantics "navigate the camera/active context into space X," not "pass data." Concretely:

  • Space (Scene in the ontology) — a named container with its own coordinate canvas and subgraph.
  • Portal — an edge of type portal with { from: nodeId, to: spaceId, transition }. The transition animates the camera (Pad++ style) or switches the active space.
  • This keeps the data graph acyclic and small, while the navigation graph (spaces connected by portals) is a separate, possibly-cyclic graph you traverse interactively. Separating the data/dependency graph and the navigation graph is the key architectural move: they're different graphs over (partly) the same nodes.

6. Ontology and mapping to implementation (TypeScript / web stack)

Comparison of node-editor libraries.

  • Rete.js — a "TypeScript-first framework for node-based editors"; it has solutions for both dataflow and control-flow, with render plugins for React/Vue/Angular/Svelte. Best when you want a built-in processing model and TS-first.
  • LiteGraph.js — a graph engine+editor with its own HTML5 Canvas2D; runs on client/server, exports the graph to JSON. Self-contained, battle-tested (the basis of ComfyUI).
  • React Flow / XYFlow — a customizable React render/interaction layer, not a computation engine — execution is your own.
  • baklavajs — a graph/node editor with a Vue focus (matching your stack) + a separate engine package.
  • nodl — a small TypeScript/RxJS reactive framework (data as observable streams) — interesting if you want push-based FRP out of the box.

Recommendation: for your Vue/TS stack and your desire to own the model — either (a) build the graph store yourself (it's small — schema below) and take a thin renderer (React Flow/XYFlow or baklavajs for Vue, or tldraw to get the infinite canvas + bindings for free), or (b) take Rete.js. tldraw's Workflow kit is the canonical reference even if you don't take it, because it solves coordinates+ports+bindings+execution together.

The visual transformation layer. For the "signal-as-image" medium: hydra-synth (npm) gives the modular video-synth model out of the box — the fastest path to audio-reactive graphics. For more control: regl (functional, stateless WebGL — a great fit for dataflow), PixiJS (a fast 2D sprite/filter pipeline — ideal for image changing/cycling and 2D glitch), three.js (when you need 3D). Raw WebGL/WebGPU fragment shaders are the substrate; each transform node compiles into a shader pass. For feedback nodes — ping-pong framebuffers.

The audio layer. Web Audio API for capture + AnalyserNode for FFT/waveform; Tone.js for Transport (musical time, BPM, quantized scheduling) and Tone.Analyser/Tone.Meter. Wire it up like this: AudioContext → analysis nodes (FFT/onset/RMS) → "signal" nodes analogous to CHOP in the graph; Transport/WAAClock → a Clock node that emits quantized beat/bar events.

Stitching the audio clock to the visual graph. This is where the two rate domains meet. The Clock (audio-thread precision) schedules discrete quantized events (beat, bar) via lookahead; the render loop (requestAnimationFrame, ~60 fps) pulls the visual graph every frame. Continuous audio features (bass energy) are sampled by the graph every frame (pull); discrete musical events (beat → image change) are pushed by the clock as triggers. This is the same control-rate/audio-rate bridge from Max, rewritten for the browser.

The layered ontology (Data → Structure → System → Runtime).

  • Data = the values on the edges: typed signals — Texture (GPU image/framebuffer), Signal/Channel (a number or a stream of samples, the CHOP analog), Geometry/Points, Scalar/Vector, Trigger/Event (discrete, beat-quantized), Table/Text. Define a small closed lattice of types with explicit converter nodes.
  • Structure = the graph itself: Node, Port, Edge, plus Space (canvas/Scene) and Portal (a navigation edge). A static, serializable description (a JSON Canvas-style document).
  • System = the computation engine: dirty-flag propagation, topological sorting per Space, a pull-from-sinks scheduler, rate bridges, cycle/feedback legalization (delay nodes), Transport/Clock.
  • Runtime = the per-frame tick (the Lustre "reaction"): the clock advances → scheduled events fire → the render loop pulls the active Space's sinks → dirty subgraphs "cook" → the frame is shown. This maps onto your ocraft instinct of "HTML as runtime": a Space is a DOM canvas, nodes are DOM elements with embedded behavior, the graph is a file-like model of nodes.

The data model of a node-with-behavior (an echo of the ocraft DOM-node model). A concrete minimal schema:

type PortDir = 'in' | 'out';
type SignalType = 'texture' | 'signal' | 'geometry' | 'scalar' | 'trigger' | 'table';

interface Port {
  id: string;
  dir: PortDir;
  type: SignalType;        // for type-checked connections (baklavajs/Rete do this)
  multi?: boolean;         // multi-input (Composite/Merge) vs single
}

interface Node {
  id: string;
  kind: string;            // 'osc' | 'image' | 'fft' | 'cycleOnBeat' | 'feedback' | ...
  role: 'source' | 'transform' | 'combine' | 'sink' | 'convert';
  x: number; y: number;    // free coordinate (canvas)
  spaceId: string;         // which Space it lives in
  params: Record<string, unknown>;
  state: Record<string, unknown>;  // node-local memory (for 'pre'/feedback/cycles)
  ports: Port[];
  dirty: boolean;
  // behavior:
  cook(inputs): outputs;   // pure-ish per-tick evaluation
}

interface Edge {
  id: string;
  fromNode: string; fromPort: string;
  toNode: string;   toPort: string;
}

interface Portal {         // a navigation edge, NOT a data edge
  id: string;
  fromNode: string;        // a portal node/handle
  toSpace: string;         // target Space
  transition?: 'zoom' | 'cut' | 'fade';
}

interface Space {          // Scene in his Concept System
  id: string;
  name: string;
  nodes: string[];         // node ids
  edges: string[];
  camera: { x: number; y: number; zoom: number };
}

It mirrors JSON Canvas (nodes[] with x,y + edges[] with fromNode/toNode) and tldraw (a shape with base props + typed props + a behavioral class in the ShapeUtil style), adding a Port.type system (like Rete/baklavajs), node-local state (for the pre/delay/feedback rule), and a separate navigation layer Portal/Space. It maps onto the Concept System ontology: Concept ≈ a node-kind definition, Scene ≈ Space, Service ≈ a bridge node to the outside world (audio device, MIDI, file, clock).

Recommendations

Phase 1 — a minimal working signal graph (prove the core loop).

  • Build the graph store yourself in TS (schema above) — it's small and you want to own it; render via tldraw (free canvas + ports + bindings + culling) or React Flow/baklavajs.
  • Implement pull-based computation with dirty-flags: sinks pull every requestAnimationFrame; nodes get marked dirty when a parameter/edge changes; topologically sort each Space's subgraph and "cook" in order (guarantees glitch-freedom).
  • Transformation layer: embed hydra-synth or write a small regl pipeline where each transform node = one shader pass. Set up 4–6 primitives: image/source, transform, color, blend, modulate, output.
  • Threshold to move on: a 20-node graph holds a steady 60 fps and recomputes only the dirty nodes.

Phase 2 — the audio clock and beat-synced transformation.

  • Add one Transport/Clock node on Tone.js Transport or WAAClock over AudioContext.currentTime with two-clocks lookahead (25 ms interval, 100 ms lookahead); expose BPM (entered/tapped) and support Ableton Link.
  • Add analysis nodes: FFT (bass/mid/treble bands), RMS/envelope, onset (spectral flux), beat/BPM. Continuous features are pulled every frame; discrete beat/bar events are pushed as a trigger.
  • Build a cycleOnBeat node (advances an image index, quantized to beat/bar) — this directly delivers the "change images on the beat" requirement.
  • Threshold: image changes land within one frame of the beat at 120–160 BPM; a tempo change doesn't break sync.

Phase 3 — feedback, generative primitives, and the navigation layer.

  • Add a Feedback node (ping-pong framebuffers) and introduce the cycle rule: reject cycles unless they pass through a delay/feedback node.
  • Add 1–2 generators: an L-system node (alphabet/rules/axiom → geometry) for trees/structures and a CA/noise node. Build an expressive-range view (sample many outputs, plot 2 metrics like density/complexity) to see and tune the generative space and avoid the Kaleidoscope Effect.
  • Implement Spaces + Portals: separate the data graph (per Space, acyclic) and the navigation graph (Spaces connected by portals, may be cyclic). A portal transition animates the camera (a smooth Pad++-style zoom) rather than nesting.
  • Threshold: you can create two Spaces, connect them with a portal, and transition with animation; a feedback chain gives stable video feedback without blowing up.

Phase 4 — make it a sandbox for others.

  • Add serialization to a JSON Canvas-style document (portable, diffable).
  • Constrain deliberately (Stravinsky): ship a small curated set of primitives, a fixed palette (Ikeda/Alva Noto monochrome + an accent), and beat-quantization on by default — constraints concentrate the meaningful space and make the first outputs good right away.
  • Add MIDI-in (WebMIDI) and an "anything-to-anything" modulation-routing UI (the VDMX model) so others can patch expressively.

What would change these recommendations: if you need guaranteed determinism/reproducibility (render-to-file, networked collaboration) — go with fixed-rate SDF scheduling or KPN semantics instead of best-effort pull. If performance on hundreds of nodes becomes the bottleneck — move from per-node shader passes to a single compiled shader per chain (the Hydra/Houdini-VOP model): compile the graph into GLSL rather than interpreting node-by-node.

Caveats

  • Pull vs push is a real trade-off, not free. Pure pull (TouchDesigner) is ideal for frame-driven visuals but awkward for discrete events; pure push is the opposite. You'll arrive at a hybrid (pull for continuous frame data, push/triggers for beats) — budget for the bridge logic, where the subtle timing bugs live.
  • Glitch-freedom requires a topological sort every time the graph or values branch into diamonds; skip it and you'll get intermittent, hard-to-reproduce artifacts. For small graphs it's cheap — do it from day one.
  • Web Audio timing precision varies across browsers (Spectre/Meltdown mitigations may coarsen timer resolution); a lookahead scheduler mitigates but doesn't eliminate jitter. Test on the target browser/OS.
  • Beat/onset detection is imperfect on dense, sub-heavy techno; multi-band onset helps, but for the tightest sync prefer Ableton Link / MIDI clock from Ableton over blind audio analysis when you're the source.
  • A "huge meaningful space" isn't guaranteed by a large number of primitives — the Kaleidoscope Effect is the central risk. Validate with expressive-range analysis and lean on constraints; perceptual diversity matters, not combinatorial capacity.
