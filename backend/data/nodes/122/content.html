great action is outwardly unstable. what we take for stability turns out to be a deception, something like an astonishing magic trick.

in our striving for the effective, we substitute the part for the whole, unable to see that, by winning (winning effectively!) at the level of the part, at the level of the whole we suffer a crushing defeat. Let me give just one example: the discovery and use of antibiotics ultimately led to the emergence of strains of bacteria that are absolutely resistant to them

Laozi proposes a fundamentally different approach: in order for our action to proceed from a vision of the whole rather than from a deceptive perception of the partial, we must renounce goal-setting, willful action.

my direct action is my arbitrary will, the reverse action is the force of the Dao

What, then, is this mysterious "reverse action"? Laozi does not speak of it directly; rather, he only hints at it. For him, reverse action is "neither to lose nor to struggle." In other words, to remain in search of the subtlest, dynamic balance between opposing forces. According to Laozi, balance acts above causes and effects, nonlocally, instantly bringing all elements of reality into accord as a single whole — which is why "the Dao does not struggle, yet it wins."

Laozi put it this way: "to take from what is lacking and give to what is in excess — this is the human way". Taiji offers what Laozi called the Heavenly way: "to take from what is in excess and give to what is in want". Only such a way leads to harmony and to the growth of possibilities
