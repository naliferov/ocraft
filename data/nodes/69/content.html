basics of differential calculus, gradients, derivatives (important when studying optimization algorithms such as gradient descent)

Data for good datasets - big hunt. big companies sometimes open their algorithms, but datasets seldom

Features (data markup) — can be a car's mileage, user sex, price, even a counter for how often a word appears in a text

Good when data structured for example (tables). But what if we have GBs of cats pics? When there are many features, the model works inefficiently. Often selecting the right features takes more time than the rest of the training. But there are situations where a person picks the features they consider "right" and introduces subjectivity into the model, and it starts lying.

Algorithms A single task can be solved by different methods. The choice of method affects accuracy, speed, and the size of the finished model. But if the data is garbage, even the best algorithm won't help. Don't fixate on percentages — better to collect more data.

Deep learning — a neural-network architecture, one of the approaches to building and training them. In practice, few people today distinguish where the networks are deep and where they aren't. They just say the name of the specific network and that's it.

Classical learning

The first algorithms came to us from pure statistics back in the 1950s. They solved formal problems — they looked for patterns in numbers, estimated how close points were in space, and computed directions.

Today they power roughly half of the internet. When you come across a "Recommended articles" block on a site, or the bank freezes the money on your card after your first coffee purchase abroad — it's almost always the work of one of these algorithms.

Yes, big corporations love to solve every problem with neural networks. Because an extra 2% of accuracy means plus 2 billion. Everyone else should use their head. When a task is solvable by classical methods, it's cheaper to implement a system that's at least somewhat useful for the business with them, and only then think about improvements.

Classical algorithms are simple, and like the basics of arithmetic, they're needed all the time.

Supervised learning

Classical learning is usually split into two categories. You'll often see their English names — Supervised and Unsupervised Learning

In the first case, the machine has a kind of teacher who tells it what's correct. That is, the teacher has already divided (labeled) all the data into cats and dogs in advance, and the machine learns from specific examples.

In unsupervised learning, the machine is simply dumped a pile of animal photos on the table and told "figure out who looks like whom here." The data isn't labeled, the machine has no teacher, and it tries to find any patterns on its own. We'll talk about these methods below.

Obviously, with a teacher the machine learns faster and more accurately, which is why it's used far more often in real-world tasks. These tasks are divided into two types: classification — predicting an object's category, and regression — predicting a place on the number line.

Classification

Today it's used for:

  • Spam filters
  • Language detection
  • Finding similar documents
  • Sentiment analysis
  • Recognizing handwritten letters and digits
  • Detecting suspicious transactions

Classification always needs a teacher — labeled data with features and categories that the machine will learn to determine from those features
