the not-I object limits the I subject

The "not-I" limits the "I", but in doing so it imparts an impulse (Anstoss), a check on the I's infinite striving for mastery.

The "I" strives to overcome this check. Within it awaken drives toward reflection — but reflection necessarily presupposes the realization of some object of representation — and the "I" manifests this striving toward productivity. Yet the I's attempt to embody its practical strivings, to slake its insatiable thirst for activity, runs up against the limitation posed by the "not-I". Hence dissatisfaction, a feeling of constraint; it gives rise to a striving toward self-determination. Self-determination must consist in freedom, in harmony between drive and its realization. Such harmony is attainable only through unceasing activity for the sake of activity, in which the "absolute drive" of our "I" is expressed — moral duty.
